T.U.P.A.

T.U.P.A. stands for Toolbox for Unsanctioned Public Art. T.U.P.A. is composed of a series of pieces. This project was a continuation of work I had done within the domain of graffiti. To explore the domain of graffiti more fully I felt I needed to create a series of pieces. This series would explore the ways in which I could create pieces that would grab the attention of the public using one of the five senses. By doing this I would begin to create an intellectual toolbox. This toolbox could then be used in the creation of more effective public art whether it was sanctioned, or unsanctioned.

Sight

The first piece in this series was intended to take advantage of the sense of sight. It was installed outside of a popular night club in an industrial area of Bushwick. It was made from a bracket I fabricated by welding 1/2" steel pipe together to create a frame to support the disco-ball and glitter containers. I attached the piece to a street light using ratchet straps. Glitter was released by pulling on the rope attached to each of the boxes.

Smell

The second piece in this series was intended to take advantage of the sense of smell. It was installed taking advantage of scaffolding that is common throughout New York. This piece was installed in December. One of the smells I most commonly associate with December is that of pine. By soaking 1"x1" balsa wood squares in a mixture of silver fir, and cedar essential oils I wanted to take full advantage of the time of year and the smells associated with it. By using smell as a cue I hoped passers by would take a moment to hang an ornament I provided on a mock Christmas tree and take a candy cane.

Unfortunately this did not happen. With in 15 minutes of installation all of the candy canes were taken. After another two hours all of the decorations had been taken as well. I learned a lot from making and installing this piece. When installing public art, if it is sanctioned or not, weather must be taken into account. I didn't realize that cold weather makes smells weaker. Because the air temperature was near freezing people passing by were not able to smell the scent I had created so the Christmas tree cutout with ornaments and candy canes most likely made very little sense. I also learned that any part of an installation that is not tied down is very likely to be taken. With all of this in mind I turned to making the final piece in this series.

Sound

This piece was designed to take advantage of the auditory sense, as well as the ambient energy available in subway stations. Using 1/2" electrical conduit pipe and cutting at the proper lengths I made chimes that correspond to notes on the musical scale. The chimes were then installed near the edge of the subway platform so the energy created by the arriving trains would cause them to hit each other.

As you can see there was not enough energy created by the arriving train. Despite the chimes being very light and close to the platform they did not strike against each other. Not to be easily defeated I decided to place the chimes as close to the platform as I felt safe.

Again, despite being as close to the arriving train as I felt was safe the chimes did not strike against each other. I still would like to explore ways to harness the ambient energy created by the New York subway system. The first way to do this would be to investigate lighter materials to use to make chimes. It would also be interesting to learn how to accurately measure the force created by the incoming and outgoing trains.
