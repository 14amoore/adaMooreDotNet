Overview:

My partner works in the food and drink industry, because of this we are lucky enough to have an ever expanding wine collection. Wine is best stored in a cool, dark place. The area in our apartment best fitting that description is a small coat closet. While a coat closet is dark, and cool enough, it does not lend itself to easy examination of our inventory. To see what is in the back of the closet we have to unload everything, because of this we often forget what exactly is living back there.

Closet Cav is a full stack web app, using node.js, reach router, firebase as the database, react.js and react bootstrap for the front-end. Users are authenticated using email and password. I chose firebase because it is a realtime database that my partner and I can access at the same time, when one of us adds or deletes a bottle the other sees these changes happen immediately. I chose react and react bootstrap due to their functionality, and popularity within the front-end development industry.

The Problem:

Closet Cav was developed to show us what wine is currently in our coat closet by simply looking at our phones or computers. We needed to be able to enter and delete wines from inventory, and search for wines based on color, or country of origin.

This app was created for the use my partner and I, and also as a proof of concept project to be shown to other members of the food and drink industry for further UX research.

After settling on the tech stack, my partner and I spoke about the features she would like. At its most basic I knew it would be a C.R.U.D. (create, read, update, delete) app. I found that wine is a little more complicated than your average blog, or reminder app, there is a lot of information about wine that needs to be entered into any inventory. This is because of how sprawling and complicated the wine world can be. After much talk about the aspects of wine she found most pertinent we settled on color, country of origin, producer, grape varietal(s), and vintage as the most important information.

The next challenge to tackle was how the information was to be entered. I chose dropdown menus for color, and country of origin. I chose to give users a predetermined set of options for color and country because those options are the most limited and it made the process of adding wines to the app faster and more accurate. With options there‚Äôs no need to worry about spelling. The three other pieces of information, producer name, varietals, and vintage must be typed in by the user.

There are thousands of producers in France alone, grape varietals are equally numerous so it made sense to ask the user to type this information in. Some wines, mainly fortified wines, do not have a specific vintage printed on their bottle, users must either enter a four digit number or nothing at all. If the user attempts to enter something other than a four digit number an error message is displayed. You can see the final UI below.

Outcomes and Lessons:

For the two of us this app works very well. Now, when we‚Äôre deciding what to open we know exactly what our options are, most importantly we don‚Äôt need to unpack the whole closet. In a field as large as wine there is a lot of information people feel is important to include when creating an inventory of their collection. The most important piece of information I did not include in this iteration was region, and sub-region for each of the countries. Regions and sub-regions are most important in well known wine producing countries like Italy, France, and the USA.

After showing this app to people who work across the spectrum of the food and beverage industry they had a lot of suggestions for improvement I would like to incorporate. The top three being, a favorites list, conditionally rendered input sections for regions and sub-regions, and a way to add more than one bottle at a time.

Conditional rendering is one of the great advantages of front-end frameworks like react. A favorites list is a great way for users to keep track of what they like, and the ability to add more than one bottle when entering wine would be very useful for people who tend to buy multiple bottles from the same vintage.

I'm going to keep researching and working on this project because of the positive feedback I've gotten from the people I've shown this too.
